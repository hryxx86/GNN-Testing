# GNN-Based Cross-Sectional Stock Ranking with Dynamic Graphs — Progress Report (v3)

## Executive Summary

This report documents the development and evaluation of a Graph Neural Network pipeline for S&P 500 stock ranking prediction. The project began with binary direction prediction (up/down), which failed completely — all models, including GNN variants, XGBoost, and GPT-4o-mini structured sentiment, produced AUC indistinguishable from random chance. A literature-driven pivot to cross-sectional ranking prediction yielded three principal findings.

First, a GAT model operating on correlation and sector edges achieved IC=0.044 at the 21-day horizon, exceeding the 0.03 significance threshold and producing an annualized net long-short return of 15.1% after transaction costs. This confirms that GNN graph structure carries genuine, non-redundant predictive information for stock ranking.

Second, a systematic six-horizon ablation (1d/5d/10d/21d/42d/63d) revealed that the predictive value of graph structure is horizon-dependent. GNN outperforms flat-feature baselines by 2.8–2.9 times at 10–21 day horizons, but provides zero benefit at 1-day or beyond 42 days. Meanwhile, LightGBM's IC increases monotonically with horizon. This divergent cross-pattern — graph models peaking at medium horizons while flat-feature models peak at long horizons — has not been reported in the existing literature.

Third, SelectiveNet (ICML 2019) fails in the financial GNN context. The learned selection head produces negatively correlated confidence scores at all coverage levels, selecting the worst predictions rather than the best. However, the three-head architecture's auxiliary loss provides beneficial regularization, yielding the highest IC of all experiments (0.056).

Four of five minimum publication criteria are met. The remaining work centers on walk-forward cross-validation to address GNN training instability (GAT IC showed 105% coefficient of variation across two runs).

## 1. Research Context

This project investigates whether Graph Neural Networks combined with financial news can improve stock prediction on the S&P 500. The hypothesis is that cross-stock information propagated through correlation and sector graphs provides predictive signal beyond what individual stock features alone can capture. The computing environment uses Google Colab with NVIDIA A100 GPU. Core libraries are PyTorch, PyTorch Geometric, FinBERT, LightGBM, and scikit-learn.

Phases 1 and 2, covered in the previous report, established two foundations. Phase 1 built and analyzed the S&P 500 correlation network, identifying hub stocks dominated by Industrials and Financials sectors. Phase 2 demonstrated a small-scale news-driven GNN prototype on 9 hub stocks, where GraphSAGE outperformed a text-only baseline by 3.4% in AUC. The present report covers the scale-up to the full S&P 500, the failure of binary direction prediction, the literature-driven pivot to ranking, and the resulting experiments.

## 2. Data Infrastructure

### News Pipeline and Feature Construction

Following the recommendation from the February 20 meeting, the manual Factiva process was replaced with an automated EODHD API pipeline. The raw dataset contains 1,698,182 news events spanning January 2021 to December 2025. After mapping to S&P 500 tickers, 1,538,967 events were retained (90.6% match rate). Each article was encoded using FinBERT (ProsusAI/finbert), producing a 768-dimensional embedding and a 3-dimensional sentiment vector.

A critical design change was the shift from event-driven to calendar-driven data organization. Rather than generating predictions only when news appears, every stock receives a prediction on every trading day — the paradigm used by all recent state-of-the-art papers. For each of the 1,255 trading days and 501 stocks, a 781-dimensional feature vector was constructed: 9 price-derived features (returns, volatilities, and momentum over 5/10/21-day windows, all computed from T-1 close to prevent leakage), 768 FinBERT embedding dimensions (mean-pooled across same-day articles), 3 sentiment scores, and 1 has-news indicator. The complete feature tensor has shape (1255, 501, 781) and occupies 1.96 GB.

### Dynamic Graph Construction

Following the first action item from the February 20 meeting, a systematic sensitivity analysis of the correlation graph was conducted. Three rolling window lengths (63, 126, 252 trading days) were crossed with four thresholds (0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7), producing 12 configurations. The optimal configuration is window=126 days with threshold=0.6, selected based on five criteria: moderate density (~6%), acceptable fragmentation (125 connected components), balanced clustering (coefficient 0.453), temporal stability (density standard deviation 0.064, three times more stable than the 63-day window), and clear regime sensitivity (edge count spikes during the 2022 sell-off). This produced 54 monthly correlation snapshots. Only price data through December 2024 was used for graph construction to prevent future information leakage. Static sector edges (27,070 edges across 11 GICS sectors) were also constructed.

### Prediction Labels and Time Split

For each of six horizons (1, 5, 10, 21, 42, 63 trading days), forward returns were computed as close-to-close percentage changes, then converted to cross-sectional z-scores by subtracting the daily market mean and dividing by the daily cross-sectional standard deviation. This removes the market beta confound that dominated the earlier binary labels. The data was split chronologically: training from July 2021 through December 2023 (629 days), validation from January through June 2024 (124 days), and test from July 2024 through January 2026 (396 days).

## 3. Why Binary Direction Prediction Failed

### The AUC ≈ 0.50 Result

Six configurations were tested for next-day binary direction prediction: logistic regression with FinBERT embeddings, logistic regression with sentiment, and four GNN variants with progressively richer edge types. All produced test AUC between 0.49 and 0.51, indistinguishable from random chance.

### Diagnosis

Data-level diagnostics revealed that 26.5% of next-day returns fall within a noise zone of less than 0.5% magnitude. FinBERT's positive sentiment aligned with positive returns only 51.6% of the time — barely above random. Three signal fixes were applied: news deduplication (1.7M events reduced to 437K unique stock-days), market-adjusted labels (noise zone reduced from 27.6% to 23.0%), and momentum features (5/10/21-day returns and volatilities). After all fixes, the best test AUC was 0.5046 from XGBoost. Selective prediction at 5% coverage produced AUC=0.5154, well within noise.

### LLM Validation

GPT-4o-mini structured sentiment was tested on a 7,000-event holdout. It produced AUC=0.5034, only 0.0009 above FinBERT. The high-impact event subset performed worst (AUC=0.4762), suggesting the market prices high-impact news fastest. This confirmed that the barrier is not model capacity but the task itself: next-day binary direction prediction on S&P 500 large caps is infeasible with NLP features, consistent with the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

## 4. Research Pivot: From Binary Classification to Cross-Sectional Ranking

A survey of 10 recent papers revealed that every major financial GNN paper published in 2022–2025 — MASTER (AAAI 2024), FinMamba (2025), MDGNN (AAAI 2024), THGNN (CIKM 2022) — uses ranking-based evaluation with IC and Sharpe metrics rather than binary classification. The redesign adopted three changes: the prediction target became cross-sectional z-score returns instead of binary labels; the evaluation metrics became IC, ICIR, and long-short portfolio Sharpe instead of AUC; and the data paradigm became calendar-driven instead of event-driven.

The evaluation framework uses three complementary metrics. The Information Coefficient (IC) is the daily cross-sectional Spearman rank correlation between predicted scores and actual z-score returns, where IC above 0.03 is considered meaningful. The IC Information Ratio (ICIR) measures signal stability as mean IC divided by IC standard deviation. The long-short portfolio backtest goes long the top 30 ranked stocks and short the bottom 30, with 15 basis points per round-trip deducted as transaction costs.

The survey also identified a gap: no existing paper combines dynamic multi-edge-type graphs, news and price multimodal features, ranking prediction, selective prediction, and systematic multi-horizon ablation. This combination defines the project's contribution space.

## 5. Ranking Pipeline: Architecture and Baselines

### Non-GNN Baselines

Four baselines were established on the 5-day default horizon. Ridge regression with 9 price features (B1) produced IC=0.005. Ridge with all 781 features (B2) produced IC=0.005, showing that adding 772 news dimensions to a linear model provides negligible improvement. XGBoost (B3) produced IC=0.003. LightGBM (B4) achieved IC=0.008 with Sharpe=0.773, the best non-GNN result.

### GNN Models

Two GNN families were implemented. RankingHGT is a Heterogeneous Graph Transformer operating on a heterogeneous graph with stock and news node types and four edge types (correlation, sector, mentions, co-occurrence). It learns separate key/query/value projections per edge type. RankingGNN operates on a homogeneous graph where edge types are merged, tested with both GraphSAGE (mean aggregation) and GAT (4 attention heads, 16 dimensions each). Both architectures use 2 layers, hidden dimension 64, LayerNorm, residual connections, and 0.3 dropout. Training uses Adam (lr=1e-3, weight decay=1e-4), gradient accumulation over 32 days, early stopping with patience 15, and MSE loss on z-score labels.

### GNN Ablation: Simpler Wins

Five GNN configurations were tested, varying architecture and edge composition. The results across two independent runs reveal two critical patterns.

First, news and co-occurrence edges are harmful. The full HGT model with all four edge types was the worst GNN configuration in both runs, with IC between 0.004 and 0.009 and negative Sharpe ratios. These edges create dense, noisy connections (averaging 1,226 mentions and 2,325 co-occurrences per day) that dilute the informative correlation structure.

Second, simpler architectures outperform complex ones. Both GAT and SAGE on homogeneous graphs with only correlation and sector edges consistently outperformed HGT on heterogeneous graphs. HGT's type-specific attention parameters are designed to distinguish between edge types, but this distinction adds noise in the weak-signal regime where 98% of variation is unpredictable.

### Training Stability

Comparing the two runs exposed a significant stability concern. Non-GNN baselines produced identical results, confirming code correctness. SAGE showed excellent stability (IC coefficient of variation ~2%). HGT showed moderate variation (CV 15–69%). GAT was the least stable: IC swung from 0.021 to 0.006 across runs (CV ~105%). This instability arises from daily sequence shuffling, gradient accumulation boundaries, early stopping at different epochs, and CUDA non-determinism.

## 6. The Horizon Effect: GNN's Temporal Sweet Spot

### Experimental Design

The horizon ablation is the primary novel contribution of this work. No existing GNN stock prediction paper has systematically compared prediction performance across multiple time horizons. Separate GAT models were trained for each of six horizons (1, 5, 10, 21, 42, 63 trading days). LightGBM was trained on the same horizons as the flat-feature comparator.

### The Inverted-U Pattern

The GAT model's IC forms a clear inverted-U curve: 1d at -0.001, 5d at 0.023, 10d at 0.039, 21d at 0.044, 42d at -0.009, and 63d at -0.008. The peak at 21 days (IC=0.044, ICIR=0.374) exceeds the 0.03 significance threshold. The 10-day horizon also crosses this threshold at IC=0.039. Both the 1-day and 42–63 day horizons produce negative IC values, indicating that graph structure provides no useful information at these extremes.

LightGBM exhibits an entirely different pattern: IC increases monotonically across horizons, from 0.004 at 1 day to 0.052 at 63 days. LightGBM achieves its best performance at the longest horizon, precisely where GAT fails.

### Why the Patterns Diverge

The divergence reveals that graph-propagated cross-stock information and individual stock features capture fundamentally different predictive signals. At the 1-day horizon, noise dominates and neighbor aggregation amplifies rather than filters it. At 5–21 days, cross-stock information transmitted through correlation and sector edges carries genuine predictive value — GAT outperforms LightGBM by 2.8–2.9 times in this range. At 42–63 days, long-term trends are driven by macroeconomic factors and company fundamentals better captured by momentum features in LightGBM's flat input; the local graph topology becomes irrelevant.

After deducting 15 basis points per round-trip, only GAT at 10-day (annualized net return 11.7%) and 21-day (15.1%) horizons produce economically significant returns. LightGBM produces meaningful net returns only at 42-day (8.5%) and 63-day (24.4%) horizons, though the 63-day model has extreme maximum drawdown (410%).

## 7. Selective Prediction with SelectiveNet

### Architecture

SelectiveNet (Geifman and El-Yaniv, ICML 2019) extends the GAT backbone with three heads: a ranking head producing the stock score, a selection head outputting a confidence value between 0 and 1 indicating whether the prediction should be trusted, and an auxiliary head providing regularization. The selection head receives the 64-dimensional stock embedding concatenated with a 4-dimensional market context vector (21-day volatility, 63-day drawdown, 30-day cross-sectional volatility, 5-day market breadth — all from T-1 data). Training proceeds in two stages: first the full model trains for up to 100 epochs, then the backbone is frozen and only the selection head trains for 50 epochs with coverage penalty (lambda=32, target=20%).

### The Selection Head Failed

At 100% coverage (no abstention), the SelectiveRankingGAT achieved IC=0.056, ICIR=0.463, and annualized net return of 16.5% — the highest IC of all experiments, suggesting the auxiliary loss provides beneficial regularization. However, the selection mechanism itself failed completely. At the target 20% coverage, SelectiveNet produced IC=-0.024: the selected subset was worse than random. At 5% and 10% coverage, IC was similarly negative. The selection head learned to anti-select, assigning higher confidence to less accurate predictions.

A simple threshold baseline selecting stocks with the highest absolute ranking scores performed as expected: IC=0.031 at 20% coverage and IC=0.051 at 50% coverage. The Jaccard overlap between threshold-selected and SelectiveNet-selected stocks ranged from only 0.20 to 0.35, confirming that SelectiveNet selects fundamentally different — and worse — stocks. The likely causes include the two-stage training disconnect (freezing the backbone prevents the selection head from co-adapting), insufficient coverage penalty, and the absence of a direct learning signal connecting selection quality to prediction accuracy.

## 8. Summary and Publication Readiness

### Key Findings

Six findings have emerged from this work.

Binary direction prediction on S&P 500 is infeasible with NLP features. No configuration — FinBERT, XGBoost, GPT-4o-mini, or any GNN architecture — achieved next-day AUC above 0.51.

The ranking approach succeeds where binary classification fails. Cross-sectional z-score prediction yields IC values up to 0.056 and economically significant long-short returns.

GNN graph structure provides genuine signal, but only at specific horizons. The 10–21 day range shows GAT outperforming LightGBM by 2.8–2.9 times. This horizon dependence has not been previously reported.

Simpler GNN architectures outperform complex ones in the weak-signal financial regime. GAT and SAGE on homogeneous graphs beat HGT on heterogeneous graphs. News and co-occurrence edges are harmful.

The GAT/LightGBM horizon cross-pattern constitutes a novel finding. Graph-based models peak at medium horizons while flat-feature models improve monotonically with longer horizons, revealing that the two model families capture complementary types of predictive information.

SelectiveNet's selection mechanism fails in the financial GNN setting, but its three-head architecture provides the best overall prediction through auxiliary regularization.

### Publication Criteria Assessment

Against five minimum criteria, four are met. IC exceeds 0.03 at the 21-day horizon (0.044). GNN IC exceeds LightGBM IC by 2.9 times at 21 days. The long-short portfolio produces 15.1% annualized net return. The horizon ablation shows a clear inverted-U pattern. The unmet criterion is selective prediction: SelectiveNet failed, and threshold selection at 20% coverage (IC=0.031) falls below the full model's IC (0.056).

### Remaining Work

Walk-forward cross-validation is the highest priority. GAT's 105% IC coefficient of variation across two runs means the 21-day IC=0.044 result requires confirmation through multiple rolling windows (2-year train, 6-month validation, 6-month test) reporting IC means and standard deviations. A decision is needed on the SelectiveNet contribution: attempt improvement through joint training and stronger coverage penalty, pivot to threshold selection as the contribution, or report the failure as a negative finding. Paper figures including the horizon ablation panels, cumulative return charts, and coverage-IC curves need finalization.

## Appendix: Complete Parameter Summary

The stock universe covers 501 S&P 500 constituents over 1,255 trading days from January 2021 to January 2026. News data comes from the EODHD API with 1,538,967 mapped events. Text encoding uses FinBERT producing 768-dimensional embeddings and 3-dimensional sentiment scores. The total feature dimension per stock-day is 781.

Dynamic correlation graphs use a rolling window of 126 trading days, Pearson correlation threshold of 0.6, and monthly re-estimation with step size 21 days, producing 54 snapshots. Sector edges are static within 11 GICS sectors totaling 27,070 edges.

GNN architecture parameters are: hidden dimension 64, 2 layers, 4 attention heads (GAT), dropout 0.3, learning rate 1e-3, weight decay 1e-4, gradient accumulation over 32 days, maximum 100 epochs, early stopping patience 15, and ReduceLROnPlateau with factor 0.5 and patience 5.

Portfolio evaluation uses top-k=30 stocks for long and short legs, 15 basis points round-trip transaction cost, and annualization factor 252/horizon.

SelectiveNet uses target coverage 0.2, coverage penalty lambda 32.0, Stage 1 training for up to 100 epochs, Stage 2 for 50 epochs with learning rate multiplied by 0.1, and 4-dimensional market context features computed from T-1 data.

The time split is: training from July 2021 to December 2023 (629 days), validation from January 2024 to June 2024 (124 days), and test from July 2024 to January 2026 (396 days).
